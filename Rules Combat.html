Combat Rules

On Your Turn

Play during combat proceeds in turns with one player after another. During your turn, you may do one of the following five plays: move then attack, or attack then move, or idle which is neither moving nor attacking. You may also move without attacking or attack without moving.

Some tabletop games allow you to split your move action, allowing you to move, then attack, then move again. In Mechanized, this requires a unique subsystem.

Moving

Each Mech has a movement drive, which gives a speed measured in spaces. On each of your turns, if you chose to move, you may move up to that number of spaces on unobstructed terrrain. For example, if you have a move speed of 3, you could follow the red path pictured to the right.

Some Terrain obstructs your movement, which means that it costs more of your speed to move through it. For example, the light forest pictured in the gold path to the right penalizes movement by 1 extra movement. As shown, the mech with a move speed of 3 can only move 2 hexes along the gold path, since moving through the light forest took an extra space. Some terrain requires even more movement than 1 extra to move through. See the Terrain Guide rules page for specifics.

You cannot move through spaces occupied by enemies. If you are playing a team game, then you can move through friendly spaces with no additional penalty.

As long as you have some amount of movement left at the end of your move, you can use it up to enter a hex of passable terrain. Consider the third step along the purple path shown to the right. Though the mech only has a speed of 3, and even though the mech used 2 of its 3 speed getting to that second hex, it can still move into that light forest as long as it ends its movement there.

See the Terrain Guide page for specific details on the penalties or bonuses to movement that each kind of terrain provides.

Different terrain offers different penalties not only to movement, but also to your accuracy and evasion. Where you move to can often be the difference between victory or blowing up! If you want your mech to move faster, you can buy upgrades to your movement drive subsystem during the build phase.

Firing Weapons

All weapons can only fire once per turn. Missiles can be fire at multiple targets in a turn, but not the same target multiple times.

To attack another player's mech, choose which weapon you are attacking with, then choose which mech you are attacking. You may only choose an opponent's mech if it is a number of spaces equal to or less than the maximum range listed on the weapon's range stat (or if you have a card that says otherwise). Roll a die for accuracy, and add up your bonuses and penalties. See the "Rolling for Accuracy Section" to see if you hit or not. If you hit, then you roll for damage. See the "rolling for damage" section below. You can attack with each of your weapons each turn. You may not attack with a weapon twice on a turn. You may decide not to use a weapon on a turn, for example if you wish to preserve ammunition.

If your weapon requires ammunition to fire, you must pay the ammunition cost whether or not your attack hits.

Rolling For accuracy

To find out if your attack hit your opponenet, roll a 12 sided dice (often abreviated as d12) then add up all of your bonuses to accuracy and subtract all of your penalties (sometimes called "maluses"). Look the resulting number up on the accuracy scale below. If the number is below 5, then your attack misses, and you deal no damage. If the number calculated is higher than 4, then your attack hit, and you get to roll damage (See the "roll for damage section next").

If the calculated number is higher than 7, then not only did you hit, but you were accurate enough to hit a weak point in your target's armor! The numbers in the accuracy table below show how much extra damage EACH DICE you roll for damage gains. The damage bonus for accuracy cannot make a dice deal more than its maximum damage.

If Alice is shooting Bob with a Large Proton Laser, which deals 2d6 damage, and her accuracy roll, after penalties and bonuses totals 10, then she gets a +2 to each of the damage dice she rolls. If she rolls a 2 and a 6, then she deals 10 laser damage, since the roll of 6 cannot be improved, but the roll of 2 gets a +2 to the damage from the accuracy.

Rolling For Target Lock

Target locks (aka missile locks) are a special type of accuracy roll. You can acquire target locks at a range of 6 + computer accuracy bonus. Roll as for accuracy as normal and ignore the damage bonus. If you are successful, then you have a target lock for this turn and next turn.

With a target lock, missiles can be launched at any time during your turn (but not to the same target twice in the same turn), provided you are within the missile's maximum range. For each missile launched, you have to make a d6 missile accuracy roll. The target's terrain and any special cards are the only modifiers to a missile accuracy role. Anything other than a roll of 1 (after modifiers) is a successful hit and the target takes damage. If the target has e-pulse shielding or AMS subsystems, some or all of your successful missile hits will be shot down before the target takes damage. Rolling a 6 for missile accuracy is always a hit. Rolling a 1 is not always a miss.

Alice has a target lock on Bob, and she is within 8 range for her H10 Halon Missiles. She fires all 10 missiles. Bob is in level-2-depth water, so her missile accuracy rolls take a -2. She rolls 6, 4, 6, 2, 1, 3, 5, 4, 4, & 3 thus 6 missiles can hit, but Bob has an LMS subsystem. LMSs shoot down 1d6+2, Bob rolls a 1, therefore 3 missiles are shot down. 3 halon missiles successfully hit for a total of 3 x 3 = 9 damage.

Range Penalties

The farther a target is away from you, the harder it is to hit. Certain Mech Chassis are better at taking longer range shots. Every Mech can buy a targeting computer subsystem to make shots easier to land.

Terrain Penalties

Accuracy is calculated by drawing a line from the center of your hex, to the center of your target. You take a penalty for hexes the line passes through. If the accuracy determined by a path to a target is in question, take the questionable penalties.

you cannot shoot through more than 2 pieces of penalizing terrain. Also, you cannot shoot through heavy forest with an additonal terrain penalty. (You count the penalizing terrain limit from the enemies hex to the hexes inbetween you, not including your hex.)

See the Terrain Guide for specifics on how different types of terrain affect accuracy.

Movement Penalties

The faster you move your mech, the harder it is for your pilot to aim. On the other hand, the faster you move your mech, the harder it is for opponenets to aim at you!

Penalty to you: Attacks on your turn suffer an accuracy penalty of one less than the shortest distance between where you started your movement and where you ended it, regardless of the cost of speed of that movement. So if you moved two spaces, one space through open terrain and another through heavy forest, even though that movement cost 4 speed, you only moved 2 spaces. So your penalty to accuracy would be -1 from your movement. If you move after your attack this turn, you have no penalty to your accuracy from your movement this turn.

Penalty to opponents: The penalty to opponent's accuracy when attacking you is calculated based on the shortest distance between where you started your movement and where you ended it. The penalty to an enemy's accuracy is one less than the distance in spaces between where you started and where you ended your move. So if you moved 3 spaces, your opponenets would have a -2 to hit you.

If you were to run in a 3-space circle, the shortest distance between where you started and where you ended is 0, enemies would have no movement penalty for shooting at you, and you would have no movement penalty for shooting at them.

Rolling for damage

Each weapon has a stat for damage asociated with it (sometimes abreviated "dmg"). When you hit, you roll the dice described in the damage section of your weapon, add any bonuses you get from cards or subsystems, and then tell your opponent how much total damage you dealt and what kind of damage you dealt. Damage types can be one of the following list.

  • energy
  • missile
  • ballistic
  • melee

Energy weapons use energy but no ammo. Missile weapons have 'missile' in the name, and regular missiles cannot fire more than the number of missiles they have at the start of the round (without a special subsystem), but short range missiles can regenerate. Ballistic weapons use ammo. Melee weapons (only found in the "melee weapons" category), are adjacent-ranged, and ignore shields.

Critical Hit and Miss

Rolling a 1 is always a miss. Rolling a 12 is always a hit. Normal accuracy damage modifiers still apply.

Example

Let's say that attacker Adam is shooting a partical cannon at target Terry. Adam is in heavy forest, and Terry is in a light forest space that is 4 spaces away from Adam. There is no intervening terrain. Terry moved 3 spaces last turn, and has no jammer. Adam didn't move this turn, and has a +4 targeting computer. Adam rolls a 12 on their accuracy roll.

12 - 1 [Adam's terrain] - 1 [Terry's terrain] - 1 [range] - 2 [Terry's movement] - 0 [Adam is still] - 0 [Terry has no jammer] + 4 [Adam's targeting computer] = 11 accuracy. Looking up 11 on the accuracy table means that each damage dice that Adam rolls potentially could get a +2 to the damage!

If Adam rolls a 3 and a 5 on his two d6s, then his total damage dealt would be 11 damage, since the roll of 5 can't go over the maximum d6 roll of 6.

Idling

It takes a lot of energy to move your massive mech around and fire those huge guns. On occasion it may be tactically advantageous to use that energy for something other than moving and shooting.

Instead of moving and attacking, a player can spend their turn idling to recharge an extra +5 shield stength (not to exceed the shield's limit), recharge 1 battery by 1 turn of power, AND (if they have the card, and the nanobots are not expended) repairing with nanobots. (Passive shield regeneration can fully recharge a shield given enough time).

A player cannot use any battery at any time during a turn if they charge one of their batteries by idling that turn.

Blowing Up & Retreating

Nuclear Explosions

When your hit points drop below 1, your mech is destroyed in a nuclear explosion. To determine the size of the explosion, add the Generator Level (oft abreviated as "lvl") of all of your generators together.

The maximum distance that a nuclear explosion can deal damage is 4 spaces away from the epicenter of the explosion. Damage is reduced by 1d4 for every space away from the spaces adjacent to the epicenter. In other words, if my mech blows up adjacent to someone, they take the full nuclear explosion damage. That damage is reduced by a cumulative -1d4 for each space further away from the explosion.

Tyler's mech is standing 3 spaces away from Abby's mech when Tyler's attack destroys it. Abby had an Ion generator (level 3), two Phased Ion secondary generators (level 1 each), and a Light Hidron unique tertiary generator (level 2). Abby's total generator level is 7. Since Tyler's mech is 3 spaces away, and the 1st space takes full damage, 7 - 2 = 4, so Tyler's mech takes 4d4 nuclear damage from the explosion. Charlie's mech is 5 spaces away from Abby's, thus Charlie's mech takes no damage at all; even though Abby's generator is level 7, Charlie is out of the 4 space maximum range for a nuclear explosion's damage.

Retreating

A player may decide to retreat from combat rather than fighting it out. To retreat, the player's mech must not have had weapon accuracy/target lock rolled against them for the last 5 turns, and they must be on the edge of the map.

In terms of calculating victory and in determining order for reward cards at the end of combat, the player's mech that retreated is considered to have been knocked out of the fight at the time of retreat.

Successfuly retreating gives the player an additional +5 credits during the next build phase.

Visual Inspection

Players can visually inspect an opponent's mech's weapon class and quantity (ammo based, missile, etc.), and whether they have shields with a line of sight (LOS) of 7 hexes or less. If they are in open terrain *Please declare to everyone, or the specifc player you attack, what weapon(s) you are shooting them with.* Otherwise, LOS applies at 3 range.
