Combat Rules

On Your Turn

Play during combat proceeds in turns with one player after another. During your turn, you may do one of the following five plays: move then attack, or attack then move, or idle which is neither moving nor attacking. You may also move without attacking or attack without moving.

Some tabletop games allow you to split your move action, allowing you to move, then attack, then move again. In Mechanized, this requires a unique subsystem.

Moving

Each Mech has a movement drive, which gives a speed measured in spaces. On each of your turns, if you chose to move, you may move up to that number of spaces on unobstructed terrrain. For example, if you have a move speed of 3, you could follow the red path pictured to the right.

Some Terrain obstructs your movement, which means that it costs more of your speed to move through it. For example, the light forest pictured in the gold path to the right penalizes movement by 1 extra movement. As shown, the mech with a move speed of 3 can only move 2 hexes along the gold path, since moving through the light forest took an extra space. Some terrain requires even more movement than 1 extra to move through. See the Terrain Guide rules page for specifics.

As long as you have some amount of movement left at the end of your move, you can use it up to enter a hex of passable terrain. Consider the third step along the purple path shown to the right. Though the mech only has a speed of 3, and even though the mech used 2 of its 3 speed getting to that second hex, it can still move into that light forest as long as it ends its movement there.

See the Terrain Guide page for specific details on the penalties or bonuses to movement that each kind of terrain provides.

Different terrain offers different penalties not only to movement, but also to your accuracy and evasion. Where you move to can often be the difference between victory or blowing up! If you want your mech to move faster, you can buy upgrades to your movement drive subsystem during the build phase.

Firing Weapons

All weapons can only fire once per turn. Missiles can be fire at multiple targets in a turn, but not the same target multiple times.

To attack another player's mech, choose which weapon you are attacking with, then choose which mech you are attacking. You may only choose an opponent's mech if it is a number of spaces equal to or less than the maximum range listed on the weapon's range stat (or if you have a card that says otherwise). Roll a for accuracy, and add up your bonuses and penalties. See the "Rolling for Accuracy Section" to see if you hit or not. If you hit, then you roll for damage. See the "rolling for damage" section below. You can attack with each of your weapons each turn. You may not attack with a weapon twice on a turn. You may decide not to use a weapon on a turn, for example if you wish to preserve ammunition.

If your weapon requires ammunition to fire, you must pay the ammunition whether or not your attack hits.

Rolling For accuracy

To find out if your attack hit your opponenet, roll a 12 sided dice (often abreviated as d12) then add up all of your bonuses to accuracy and subtract all of your penalties (sometimes called "maluses"). Look the resulting number up on the accuracy scale below. If the number is below 5, then your attack misses, and you deal no damage. If the number calculated is higher than 4, then your attack hit, and you get to roll damage (See the "roll for damage section next").

If the calculated number is higher than 7, then not only did you hit, but you were accurate enough to hit a weak point in your target's armor! The numbers in the accuracy table below show how much extra damage EACH DICE you roll for damage gains. The damage bonus for accuracy cannot make a dice deal more than its maximum damage.

If Alice is shooting Bob with a Large Proton Laser, which deals 2d6 damage, and her accuracy roll, penalties and bonus total 10, then she might get a +2 to each of the damage dice she rolls. If she rolls a 2 and a 6, then she deals 10 laser damage, since the roll of 6 cannot be improved, but the roll of 2 gets a +2 to the damage from the accuracy.

Rolling for damage

Each weapon has a stat for damage asociated with it (sometimes abreviated "dmg"). When you hit, you roll the dice described in the damage section of your weapon, add any bonuses you get from cards or subsystems, and then tell your opponent how much total damage you dealt and what kind of damage you dealt. Damage types can be one of the following list.

  • laser
  • missile
  • direct fire
  • melee

Range Penalties

The farther a target is away from you, the harder it is to hit. Certain Mech Chassis are better at taking longer range shots. Every Mech can buy a targeting computer subsystem to make long range shots easier to land.

Terrain Penalties

Accuracy is calculated by drawing a line from the center of your hex, to the center of your target. You take a penalty for hexes the line passes through. If the accuracy determined by a path to a target is in question, take the questionable penalties.

you cannot shoot through more than 2 pieces of penalizing terrain. Also, you cannot shoot through heavy forest with an additonal terrain penalty. (Terrain penalties are counted up from the enemies hex to the hexes inbetween you)

See the Terrain Guide for specifics on how different types of terrain affect accuracy.

Movement Penalties

The faster you move your mech, the harder it is for your pilot to aim. On the other hand, the faster you move your mech, the harder it is for opponenets to aim at you!

Penalty to you: Attacks on your turn suffer an accuracy penalty of one less than the number of spaces you moved, regardless of the cost of speed of that movement. So if you moved two spaces, one space through open terrain and another through heavy forest, even though that movement cost 4 speed, you only moved 2 spaces. So your penalty to accuracy would be -1 from your movement. If you move after your attack this turn, you have no penalty to your accuracy from your movement this turn.

Penalty to opponents: The penalty to opponent's accuracy when attacking you is calculated based on the shortest distance between where you started your movement and where you ended it. The penalty to an enemy's accuracy is one less than the distance in spaces between where you started and where you ended your move. So if you moved 3 spaces, your opponenets would have a -2 to hit you.

If you were to run in a 3-space circle, then you would have a -2 to accuracy, but since the shortest distance between where you started an where you ended is 0, enemies would have no penalty to shooting you.

Critical Hit and Miss

Rolling a 1 is always a miss. Rolling a 12 is always a hit. Normal accuracy damage modifiers still apply.

Example

Let's say that attacker Adam is shooting a partical cannon at target Terry. Adam is in heavy forest, and Terry is in a light forest space that is 4 spaces away from Adam. There is no intervening terrain. Terry moved 3 spaces last turn, and has no jammer. Adam didn't move this turn, and has a +4 targeting computer. Adam rolls a 12 on their accuracy roll.

12 - 1 [Adam's terrain] - 1 [Terry's terrain] - 1 [range] - 1 [Terry's movement] - 0 [Adam is still] - 0 [Terry has no jammer] + 4 [Adam's targeting computer] = 12 accuracy. Looking up 12 on the accuracy table means that each damage dice that Adam rolls potentially could get a +3 to the damage!

If Adam rolls a 3 and a 4 on his two d6s, then his total damage dealt would be 12 damage, since the roll of 4 can't go over the maximum d6 roll of 6.

Idling

It takes a lot of energy to move your massive mech around and fire those huge guns. On occasion it may be tactically advantageous to use that energy for something other than moving and shooting.

Instead of moving and attacking, a player can spend their turn idling to recharge 1 additonal shield level, recharge 1 battery by 1 turn of power, AND (if they have the card, and the nanobots are not expended) repairing with nanobots. (Passive shield regeneration can fully recharge a shield given enough time).

A player cannot use any battery at any time during a turn if they charge one of their batteries by idling that turn.

Blowing Up & Retreating

Nuclear Explosions

When your hit points drop below 1, your mech is destroyed in a nuclear explosion. To determine the size of the explosion, add the Generator Level of all of your generators together.

The maximum distance that a nuclear explosion can deal damage is 4 spaces away from the epicenter of the explosion.

Tyler's mech is standing 3 spaces away from Abby's mech when tyler's attack destroys it. Abby had an Ion generator (level 3), two Phased Ion secondary generators (level 1 each), and a Light Hidron unique tertiary generator (level 2). Abby's total generator level is 7. Since Tyler's mech is 3 spaces away, 7 - 3 = 4, so Tyler's mech takes 4D4 nuclear damage from the explosion. If Charlie's mech is 5 spaces away, Charlie's mech takes no damage at all; even though Abby's generator is level 7, Charlie is out of the 4 space maximum range for a nuclear explosion's damage.

Retreating

A player may decide to retreat from combat rather than fighting it out. To retreat, the player's mech must not have had weapons used on them for the last 5 turns, and they must be on the edge of the map.

In terms of calculating victory and in determining order for reward cards at the end of combat, the player's mech that retreated is considered to have been knocked out of the fight at the time of retreat.

Successfuly retreating gives the player an additional +5 credits during the next build phase.

Visual Inspection

Players can visually inspect an opponent's mech's weapon class and quantity (ammo based, missile, etc.), and whether they have shields with line of sight (LOS), 7 hexes or less. If they are in open terrain *Please declare to everyone, or the specifc player you attack, what weapon(s) you are shooting them with.* Otherwise, LOS applies at 3 range.
